History and exam

         
Key diagnostic factors

         

            

               

                  

                     

                        

                           
                           common
                           
                           VIEW ALL
                           
                           
                           
                        

                     

                     

                        

                           
Upper abdominal pain

                        

                     

                     

                        

                           
    
                              
  • 
                                 Mid-epigastric or left upper quadrant pain that radiates to the back is the most common presenting symptom. [2] [3] [4]
                                 
      
                                    
    • more acute onset in gallstone pancreatitis than in alcoholic pancreatitis
      • 
                                    
    • typically radiates to the back straight through the middle back but can be a bandlike wraparound pattern
      • 
                                    
    • usually constant and severe but can be variable (in rare cases it is painless) “Like being stabbed with a knife”
      • 
                                    
    • typically worsens with movement, and some patients find it is eased by taking the fetal position
      • 
                                    
    • intensity and location of pain is not correlated with severity of disease. In rare cases, patients present without abdominal pain
      • 
                                 
    
                              
    • 
                              
  • 
                                 The abdominal exam may reveal a tender and distended abdomen with voluntary guarding to palpation of the upper abdomen.
                                 
      
                                    
    • Diminished bowel sounds (if an ileus has developed).
      • 
                                 
    
                              
    • 
                           

                        

                     

                     

                        

                           
Nausea and vomiting

                        

                     

                     

                        

                           
    
                              
  • 
                                 Nausea is one of the most common presenting symptoms - seen in 70% to 80% of cases.[8]
                                 
      
                                    
    • Vomiting can lead to dehydration, electrolyte abnormalities, and hypokalaemic metabolic alkalosis.[8]
      • 
                                 
    
                              
    • 
                           

                        

                     

                     

                        

                           
Signs of hypovolaemia

                        

                     

                     

                        

                           
    
                              
  • 
                                 May include decreased skin turgor, dry mucous membranes, hypotension and sweating.
                                 
      
                                    
    • In more severe cases, the patient may be tachycardic and/or tachypneic.
      • 
                                 
    
                              
    • 
                           

                        

                     

                     

                        

                           
Signs of pleural effusion

                        

                     

                     

                        

                           
    
                              
  • 
                                 Localised reduced air entry and dullness to percussion (more common on the left).
                                 
      
                                    
    • Seen in up to 50% of patients with acute pancreatitis. [13]
      • 
                                 
    
                              
    • 
                           

                        

                     

                     

                        

                           
Anorexia

                        

                     

                     

                        

                           
    
                              
  • 
                                 Decreased appetite is commonly seen
                                 
      
                                    
    • Usually secondary to nausea, pain, and general malaise.[8]
      • 
                                 
    
                              
    • 
                           

                        

                     

                     
                  

               

            

         

         
Other diagnostic factors

         

            

               

                  

                     

                        

                           
                           uncommon
                           
                           VIEW ALL
                           
                           
                           
                        

                     

                     

                        

                           
Signs of organ dysfunction

                        

                     

                     

                        

                           
    
                              
  • 
                                 It is crucial to assess for signs of early fluid loss, hypovolaemic shock and symptoms suggestive of organ dysfunction that may require immediate resuscitative measures. [2] [7] [2] [3] [4]
                                 
      
                                    
    • Look particularly for signs of cardiovascular, respiratory or renal dysfunction
      • 
                                    
    • 
                                       Assess for signs of systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS), which is defined by at least two of the following four criteria and is associated with a worse prognosis:
                                       
        
                                          
      1. Heart rate > 90 bpm
        2. 
                                          
      2. Respiratory rate > 20 breaths/min (or PaCO2 < 32 mmHg)
        3. 
                                          
      3. Temperature > 38 or < 36oC
        4. 
                                          
      4. WBC count > 12x109/L or < 4x109/L
        5. 
                                       
      
                                    
      • 
                                 
    
                              
    • 
                              
  • 
                                 Also look for other symptoms of possible organ dysfunction such as agitation and confusion.
                                 
      
                                    
    • Diminished bowel sounds (if an ileus has developed).
      • 
                                 
    
                              
    • 
                           

                        

                     

                     

                        

                           
 Dyspnoea

                        

                     

                     

                        

                           
    
                              
  •  May be present - caused by diaphragmatic splinting secondary to pain or due to pleural effusions or ARDS
    • 
                           

                        

                     

                     

                        

                           
 Jaundice

                        

                     

                     

                        

                           
    
                              
  •  In severe gallstone pancreatitis
    • 
                           

                        

                     

                     

                        

                           
 Signs of hypocalcaemia (Chvostek /Trousseau signs) (Rare)

                        

                     

                     

                        

                           
    
                              
  • 
                                 In rare cases, there may be signs of hypocalcaemia e.g.
                                 
      
                                    
    • facial muscle spasm when facial nerve is tapped ( Chvostek sign)
      • 
                                    
    • carpopedal spasm when blood pressure cuff is applied ( Trousseau sign)
      • 
                                 
    
                              
    • 
                           

                        

                     

                     

                        

                           
 Ecchymotic bruising and discolouration (Cullen /Grey-Turner /Fox signs) (Rare)

                        

                     

                     

                        

                           
    
                              
  • 
                                 Complicated hemorrhagic pancreatitis is very rare and may exhibit ecchymotic bruising and discolouration due to exudates from pancreatic necrosis.
                                 
      
                                    
    • Usually secondary to nausea, pain, and general malaise.[8]
      • 
                                    
    • 
                                       The areas that may be affected include: [5] [14]
                                       
        
                                          
      • the periumbilical skin ( Cullen sign)
        • 
                                          
      • both flanks ( Grey-Turner sign)
        • 
                                          
      • over the inguinal ligament ( Fox sign)
        • 
                                       
      
                                    
      • 
                                 
    
                              
    •
