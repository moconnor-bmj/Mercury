1st investigations to order

 Serum lipase or amylase

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              

                                 
Test

                                 
Result

                              

                              

                                 
Use serum lipase testing (if available) in preference to serum amylase [2] [15]

                                 
    
                                    
  • Serum lipase and amylase have similar sensitivity and specificity but lipase levels remain elevated for longer (up to 14 days after symptom onset vs 5 days for amylase), providing a higher likelihood of picking up the diagnosis in patients with a delayed presentation. [16]
    • 
                                 

                                 
Beware false positive and false negative results:

                                 
    
                                    
  • 
                                       Sensitivity of lipase/amylase:
                                       
      
                                          
    • 
                                             Up to one quarter of people with acute pancreatitis have lipase/amylase levels that remain within the normal range. [16]
                                             
        
                                                
      • A Cochrane review found a sensitivity of 71% for amylase and 80% for lipase, based on the accepted diagnostic threshold (>3x upper limit of normal). The review included 10 studies covering 5,056 patients presenting to the emergency department with acute-onset abdominal pain. [16]
        • 
                                             
      
                                          
      • 
                                          
    • Serum pancreatic enzyme levels may be more likely to remain in the normal range in alcohol-related acute pancreatitis and in patients with hypertriglyceridaemia (a rare cause). [2]
      • 
                                          
    • You should therefore have a low threshold for admitting patients whose symptoms are suggestive of acute pancreatitis, even if serum lipase and/or amylase tests are normal.
      • 
                                          
    • Abdominal imaging, such as contrast-enhanced computed tomography, is recommended for patients in whom there is diagnostic doubt. [2] [7]
      • 
                                       
    
                                    
    • 
                                    
  • 
                                       Specificity of lipase/amylase:
                                       
      
                                          
    • 
                                             Serum pancreatic enzyme levels can be raised in a variety of acute surgical and other conditions e.g. impaired renal function, acute appendicitis, cholecystitis, intestinal obstruction, peptic ulcer, salivary gland disease. Around one in 10 patients with acute-onset abdominal pain due to another condition will have lipase/amylase levels above the diagnostic threshold for acute pancreatitis. [2]
                                             
        
                                                
      • A Cochrane review found a specificity of 93% for amylase and 89% for lipase, based on the accepted diagnostic threshold for acute pancreatitis (>3x upper limit of normal). The review included 10 studies covering 5,056 patients presenting to the emergency department with acute-onset abdominal pain . [16]
        • 
                                             
      
                                          
      • 
                                          
    • It is therefore important to consider alternative diagnoses even if lipase/amylase level is elevated. [16]
      • 
                                          
    • Patients with diabetes have higher median lipase levels and may need a higher diagnostic threshold (>3-5x upper limit of normal). [2]
      • 
                                       
    
                                    
    • 
                                 

                                 
The accuracy of these tests decreases with time since symptom onset.

                                 
    
                                    
  • Ask for additional investigations if you still suspect acute pancreatitis, regardless of the test result. [16]
    • 
                                 

                              

                              

                                 
>3 times the upper limit of the normal range

                                 
    
                                    
  • This result confirms the diagnosis of acute pancreatitis in a patient with acute upper abdominal pain [7]
    • 
                                 

                                 

                              

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              
 FBC and differential

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              

                                 
Test

                                 
Result

                              

                              

                                 
Leukocytosis with left shift (increase in ratio of immature to mature neutrophils and macrophages) is often seen.

                                 
    
                                    
  • WBC count > 12x109/L or < 4x109/L is one of the four criteria for identifying SIRS (two or more must be present)
    • 
                                 

                                 
An elevated hematocrit (>44%) on presentation is a predictor of poor prognosis.

                                 
    
                                    
  • 
                                       The haematocrit may be elevated as a result of dehydration due to third space fluid loss.
                                       
      
                                          
    • Elevated haematocrit (>44%) is an independent predictor of severity of acute pancreatitis and the risk of developing necrotising pancreatitis. [2] [17] [18]
      • 
                                       
    
                                    
    • 
                                 

                              

                              

                                 

                                 
    
                                    
  • Leukocytosis
    • 
                                    
  • Haematocrit >44% indicates poorer prognosis
    • 
                                 

                                 

                              

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              
 C-reactive protein (CRP)

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              

                                 
Test

                                 
Result

                              

                              

                                 
Is sometimes used as an early indicator of severity and to monitor progression of inflammation. [19]

                                 
    
                                    
  • 
                                       CRP > 200 units/L is associated with pancreatic necrosis. [19]
                                       
      
                                          
    • However, there is ongoing doubt over the diagnostic test accuracy of CRP for predicting pancreatic necrosis.[20] It may take 72 hours after symptom onset to become accurate as an inflammatory marker. [2] [21]
      • 
                                       
    
                                    
    • 
                                 

                              

                              

                                 
if >200 units/L, is associated with pancreatic necrosis

                              

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              
 Urea/creatinine

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              

                                 
Test

                                 
Result

                              

                              

                                 
Elevated levels suggest dehydration/ hypovolaemia and increased risk for development of severe disease [2]

                                 
    
                                    
  • Urea > 7.14 mmol/L (or rising) or creatinine ≥ 2mg/dl is suggestive of renal failure.
    • 
                                    
  • Testing can be useful as one factor in identifying patients at increased risk of severe disease/deterioration. [2]
    • 
                                    
  • Serial measurements can aid monitoring of the patient’s response to therapy. [7]
    • 
                                 

                              

                              

                                 
Elevated in severe cases

                              

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              
 Arterial blood gas

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              

                                 
Test

                                 
Result

                              

                              

                                 
Patients may be hypoxaemic and require supplemental oxygen.

                                 
    
                                    
  • Check arterial blood gases on presentation and then every 12 hours for the first 3 days to assess both oxygenation and acid-base status. [3]
    • 
                                    
  • PaO2 < 60 mm Hg is a sign of organ failure. [2]
    • 
                                 

                              

                              

                                 

                                 
    
                                    
  • hypoxaemia and disturbances in acid-base balance
    • 
                                    
  • PaO2 < 60 mm Hg is a sign of organ failure.
    • 
                                 

                                 

                              

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              
 AST/ALT

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              

                                 
Test

                                 
Result

                              

                              

                                 
Elevated levels confirm gallstones as the cause

                                 
    
                                    
  • 
                                       ALT/AST >3x upper limit of normal predicts gallstones as the cause of acute pancreatitis. [7] [8]
                                       
      
                                          
    • Three studies have shown that an ALT level > 150 U/L within the first 48 hours after symptom onset has a positive predictive value of over 85% for gallstone pancreatitis [22] [23] [24]
      • 
                                          
    • However, the specificity for pancreatitis is low. [46]
      • 
                                       
    
                                    
    • 
                                 

                                 
In the absence of choledocholithiasis, liver function tests are usually normal

                                 
    
                                    
  • A slight increase in alkaline phosphatase and bilirubin may be seen.
    • 
                                 

                              

                              

                                 
if >3 times the upper normal limit, predicts gallstone disease as aetiology in 95% of cases

                              

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              
 Abdominal plain film

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              

                                 
Test

                                 
Result

                              

                              

                                 
Not used for diagnosis but may identify possible causative factors and/or exclude other diagnoses.

                                 
    
                                    
  • Can exclude other possible causes for presenting symptoms e.g. intestinal obstruction or perforation.
    • 
                                 

                                 
Plain abdominal x-ray is abnormal in two-thirds of patients with acute pancreatitis.

                                 
    
                                    
  • 
                                       It may reveal:
                                       
      
                                          
    • a sentinel loop (isolated dilatation of a segment of gut) adjacent to the pancreas
      • 
                                          
    • gas distending the right colon that abruptly stops in the mid- or left-transverse colon (cut-off sign)
      • 
                                          
    • calcifications.
      • 
                                       
    
                                    
    • 
                                 

                              

                              

                                 
Abnormal in two-thirds of cases

                              

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              
 CXR

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              

                                 
Test

                                 
Result

                              

                              

                                 
Not used for diagnosis but may identify possible causative factors and/or exclude other diagnoses.

                                 
    
                                    
  • 
                                       A chest x-ray may show: [25]
                                       
      
                                          
    • pleural effusion (especially in the left side)
      • 
                                          
    • basal atelectasis
      • 
                                          
    • sometimes an elevated hemidiaphragm. [25]
      • 
                                       
    
                                    
    • 
                                 

                              

                              

                                 
May show atelectasis and pleural effusion

                              

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              
 Transabdominal ultrasound

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              

                                 
Test

                                 
Result

                              

                              

                                 
Abdominal imaging is not needed for diagnosis in most patients

                                 
    
                                    
  • The diagnosis is usually made on clinical symptoms and serum lipase/amylase levels.
    • 
                                 

                                 
Request an upper right quadrant abdominal ultrasound on admission for any patient with a diagnosis of acute pancreatitis, to look for biliary aetiology [2] [7]

                                 
    
                                    
  • Early detection of gallstones will ensure a plan is put in place for their definitive management, usually by cholecystectomy, to prevent further attacks of pancreatitis and potential biliary sepsis. [2]
    • 
                                    
  • Ultrasound is inexpensive, easy to perform at the bedside, and allows examination of the gallbladder and bile duct system. Its sensitivity in detecting pancreatitis is 62% to 95%. However, it is limited by obesity and bowel gas, and is operator-dependent. [25] [26]
    • 
                                 

                              

                              

                                 

                                 
    
                                    
  • confirms or excludes gallstones
    • 
                                    
  • may show pancreatic inflammation, peri-pancreatic stranding, calcifications, or fluid collections
    • 
                                 

                                 

                              

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              
 Serum calcium

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              

                                 
Test

                                 
Result

                              

                              

                                 
Hypercalcaemia, a rare cause of acute pancreatitis, may be identified

                              

                              

                                 
Hypercalcaemia

                              

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              
 Ratio of serum lipase:amylase

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              

                                 
Test

                                 
Result

                              

                              

                                 
A serum lipase/amylase ratio > 5 favours alcohol-related pancreatitis as the cause

                                 
    
                                    
  • However, the sensitivity is low. [18]
    • 
                                 

                              

                              

                                 
>5 favours an alcohol-related cause

                              

                           

                        

                     

                  

               

            

            

               

                  

                     
Investigations to consider

                  

                  

                     

                        
                        VIEW ALL
                        
                        
                        
                     

                  

               

               

                  

                     

                        

                           

                              
 Serum triglycerides

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              

                                 
Test

                                 
Result

                              

                              

                                 
Check triglyceride levels if a gallstone or alcohol-related cause is not apparent [2]

                                 
    
                                    
  • Primary or secondary hypertriglyceridaemia is an uncommon cause of acute pancreatitis (accounting for 1-4% of episodes).
    • 
                                    
  • You should only consider it as a possible aetiology in a patient with triglyceride levels above 11.3mmol/L. [2]
    • 
                                 

                              

                              

                                 
Above 11.3mmol/L suggests hypertriglyceridaemia as a possible cause (rare)

                              

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              
 Abdominal CT scan (CECT)

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              

                                 
Test

                                 
Result

                              

                              

                                 
Abdominal imaging is not needed for diagnosis in most patients

                                 
    
                                    
  • 
                                       Most patients do not need contrast-enhanced abdominal computed tomography scanning (CECT) as diagnosis is usually based on clinical presentation and serum lipase/amylase. [7]
                                       
      
                                          
    • CECT has a sensitivity of 87-90% and specificity of 90-92% for confirming acute pancreatitis . [27]
      • 
                                          
    • There is no evidence to suggest that routine early CT improves clinical outcomes in acute pancreatitis. Conversely, there is some evidence to suggest that early (inappropriate) CT scanning has low diagnostic yield without influencing ongoing management decisions and can result in a longer hospital stay. [7] [28] [29]
      • 
                                       
    
                                    
    • 
                                 

                                 
Request CECT where there is diagnostic doubt [2] [7] [8]

                                 
    
                                    
  • 
                                       For example:
                                       
      
                                          
    • An atypical clinical presentation
      • 
                                          
    • Equivocal serum lipase/amylase results (more likely in patients who present late).
      • 
                                          
    • To rule out bowel ischaemia or intra-abdominal perforations in a patient presenting with both acute pancreatitis and acute abdomen.
      • 
                                       
    
                                    
    • 
                                 

                                 
CECT is indicated in patients who fail to improve within 48-72 hours

                                 
    
                                    
  • 
                                       Use CECT to assess disease severity and detect complications in any patient with presumed acute pancreatitis who fails to improve after 48 to 72 hours of treatment (e.g. persistent pain, fever, nausea, unable to begin oral feeding). [2]
                                       
      
                                          
    • CECT for the purpose of assessing disease severity should not be performed until 72-96 hours after the onset of symptoms, by which time the complete extent of necrosis should be visible. [7] [30]
      • 
                                       
    
                                    
    • 
                                 

                                 
Late-phase CECT (>1 week) can identify local complications

                                 
    
                                    
  • CECT is recommended in the late phase of acute pancreatitis (> 1 week after symptom onset) to look for local complications, particularly in patients with signs or symptoms of systemic disturbance or organ failure. [7] [8]
    • 
                                 

                              

                              

                                 
findings may include diffuse or segmental enlargement of the pancreas with irregular contour and obliteration of the peri-pancreatic fat, necrosis, or pseudocysts

                              

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              
 Magnetic resonance cholangiopancreatography (MRCP)

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              

                                 
Test

                                 
Result

                              

                              

                                 
Use MRCP if CECT is contraindicated (e.g. renal insufficiency, contrast allergy).

                                 
    
                                    
  • 
                                       MRI and CECT are comparable in the early assessment of acute pancreatitis. [31]
                                       
      
                                          
    • MRCP is gaining favour over CECT in some centres, due to better imaging of biliary and pancreatic stones (down to 3mm diameter), as well as better characterisation of solid versus cystic lesions. [32]
      • 
                                       
    
                                    
    • 
                                 

                                 
MRCP is preferred to CECT to screen for choledocholithiasis

                                 
    
                                    
  • Use MRCP (or EUS) to identify choledocholithiasis if it is highly suspected in the absence of cholangitis and/or jaundice. [2]
    • 
                                 

                                 
                                 
    
                                    
  • In patients with idiopathic acute pancreatitis who have had a negative routine work-up (laboratory tests, ultrasound and CECT) and negative endoscopic ultrasound, secretin-stimulated MRCP is recommended to identify rare morphologic abnormalities or pancreatobiliary tumour. [2] [7]
    • 
                                    
  • This is usually done after the patient has recovered fully from the acute phase.
    • 
                                 

                                 
                              

                              

                                 
findings may include stones, diffuse or segmental enlargement of the pancreas with irregular contour and obliteration of the peri-pancreatic fat, necrosis, or pseudocysts

                              

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              
 Endoscopic ultrasound (EUS)

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              

                                 
Test

                                 
Result

                              

                              

                                 
EUS can be used to screen for choledocholithiasis

                                 
    
                                    
  • EUS can be used as an alternative to MRCP to identify choledocholithiasis if it is highly suspected in the absence of cholangitis and/or jaundice. [2]
    • 
                                 

                                 
Consider EUS for any patient with idiopathic acute pancreatitis

                                 
    
                                    
  • 
                                       EUS is used to exclude strictures, occult biliary microlithiasis, neoplasms and chronic pancreatitis. [7]
                                       
      
                                          
    • A systematic review reported a diagnostic yield of 32-88% for EUS in detecting biliary sludge, common bile duct stones or chronic pancreatitis. The review included 5 studies, covering 416 patients with idiopathic acute pancreatitis. [33]
      • 
                                       
    
                                    
    • 
                                    
  • A patient is considered to have idiopathic acute pancreatitis after a negative routine work-up including laboratory tests (including lipid and calcium level) and imaging (transabdominal ultrasound and CECT). [7]
    • 
                                    
  • Guidelines differ on the role of endoscopic investigations in patients with idiopathic acute pancreatitis for which an aetiology remains unclear after initial laboratory and imaging tests. The International Association of Pancreatology/American Pancreatic Association 2013 guideline recommends EUS in this scenario. [7] However, the American College of Gastroenterology (ACG) guideline (also 2013) recommends limiting the use of endoscopic investigations in this patient group on the basis that the balance risks and benefits remains unclear. Where such investigations are performed, the ACG recommends they should only take place at pancreatic disease centres of excellence. [2]
    •

 Urinary trypsinogen-2

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              

                                 
Test

                                 
Result

                              

                              

                                 
Urinary trypsinogen-2 (>50ng/mL) shows promise as a diagnostic test

                                 
    
                                    
  • 
                                       Elevated urinary trypsinogen-2 (> 50 ng/mL) seems to be at least as sensitive and specific as serum lipase and amylase (both at the standard threshold of three times the upper limit of normal) for the diagnosis of acute pancreatitis. [7] [16]
                                       
      
                                          
    • Urinary trypsinogen-2 is a rapid and non-invasive bedside test but is not yet widely available for clinical use.
      • 
                                          
    • A meta-analysis reported a pooled sensitivity of 82% and specificity of 94% for diagnosing acute pancreatitis. [34]
      • 
                                       
    
                                    
    • 
                                 

                              

                              

                                 
Elevated (>50ng/mL)

                              

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              
 Serum IL-6 and IL-8

                           

                        

                        

                           

                              

                                 
Test

                                 
Result

                              

                              

                                 
Interleukin-6, interleukin-8, and interleukins-10 may be predictive markers for the development of severe acute pancreatitis. [35] [36]

                                 
    
                                    
  • One study reported a sensitivity of 81-88% and specificity of 75-85% for IL-6 and a sensitivity of 65-70% and specificity of 69-91% for IL-8for prediction of severe acute pancreatitis. [35]
    • 
                                 

                              

                              

                                 
Elevated levels may indicate increased risk of severe disease
