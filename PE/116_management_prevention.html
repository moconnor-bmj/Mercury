Primary prevention

         

            

               
               

                  
                  Specific recommendations exist for the primary prevention of venous thromboembolism (VTE) in several patient populations.
                   
                  [64] 
                  Bates SM, Greer IA, Middeldorp S, et al. VTE, thrombophilia, antithrombotic therapy, and pregnancy. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e691S-736S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60136-6/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315276?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   
                  [65] 
                  Kahn SR, Lim W, Dunn AS, et al. Prevention of VTE in nonsurgical patients. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e195S-226S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60124-X/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315261?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   
                  [66] 
                  Falck-Ytter Y, Francis CW, Johanson NA, et al. Prevention of VTE in orthopedic surgery patients. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e278S-325S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60126-3/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315265?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   
                  [67] 
                  Gould MK, Garcia DA, Wren SM, et al. Prevention of VTE in nonorthopedic surgical patients. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e227S-77S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60125-1/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315263?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   Approaches include pharmacological prophylaxis (e.g, low molecular weight heparins [LMWH], unfractionated heparin, direct-acting oral anticoagulants, fondaparinux) and mechanical thromboprophylaxis (e.g., graduated compression stockings, intermittent pneumatic compression [IPC]).
                   
                  
                  [  ] 
                  
                  How does low‐molecular‐weight heparin (LMWH) compare with unfractionated heparin (UFH) for adults with an acute medical illness and reduced mobility?
                  /cca.html?targetUrl=https://cochranelibrary.com/cca/doi/10.1002/cca.2288/full
                  Show me the answer
                  
               

               

                  
                  Inferior vena cava (IVC) filters are not recommended for the primary prevention of VTE in general surgery, abdominal-pelvic surgery, or major trauma patients.
                   
                  [67] 
                  Gould MK, Garcia DA, Wren SM, et al. Prevention of VTE in nonorthopedic surgical patients. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e227S-77S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60125-1/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315263?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   The efficacy of IVC filters for the prevention of PE remains unclear.
                   
                  [68] 
                  Kidane B, Madani AM, Vogt K, et al. The use of prophylactic inferior vena cava filters in trauma patients: a systematic review. Injury. 2012 May;43(5):542-7. 
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22386925?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   
                  [69] 
                  Bikdeli B, Chatterjee S, Desai NR, et al. Inferior vena cava filters to prevent pulmonary embolism: systematic review and meta-analysis. J Am Coll Cardiol. 2017 Sep 26;70(13):1587-97. 
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28935036?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   
                  [70] 
                  Mont MA, Jacobs JJ, Boggio LN, et al; AAOS. Preventing venous thromboembolic disease in patients undergoing elective hip and knee arthroplasty. J Am Acad Orthop Surg. 2011 Dec;19(12):768-76. 
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22134209?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   
               

               

                  
                  Non-surgical patients
               

               

                  
                  Anticoagulant thromboprophylaxis with LMWH, low-dose unfractionated heparin, or fondaparinux is recommended for acutely ill hospitalised medical patients at increased risk of VTE.
                   
                  [65] 
                  Kahn SR, Lim W, Dunn AS, et al. Prevention of VTE in nonsurgical patients. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e195S-226S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60124-X/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315261?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   
                  
                  [  ] 
                  
                  How does low‐molecular‐weight heparin (LMWH) compare with unfractionated heparin (UFH) for adults with an acute medical illness and reduced mobility?
                  /cca.html?targetUrl=https://cochranelibrary.com/cca/doi/10.1002/cca.2288/full
                  Show me the answer
                  
                   In the presence of bleeding, or if the patient is at high risk for major bleeding, mechanical thromboprophylaxis with graduated compression stockings or IPC is recommended. IPC may be preferred in hospitalised medical patients with stroke.
                   
                  [71] 
                  Dennis M, Sandercock P, Graham C, et al; CLOTS (Clots in Legs Or sTockings after Stroke) Trials Collaboration. The Clots in Legs Or sTockings after Stroke (CLOTS) 3 trial: a randomised controlled trial to determine whether or not intermittent pneumatic compression reduces the risk of post-stroke deep vein thrombosis and to estimate its cost-effectiveness. Health Technol Assess. 2015 Sep;19(76):1-90. 
                  
                  https://www.journalslibrary.nihr.ac.uk/hta/hta19760/#/abstract
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26418530?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
               

               

                  
                  Neither pharmacological nor mechanical prophylaxis is recommended for acutely ill non-surgical hospitalised patients at low risk of thrombosis.
                   
                  [65] 
                  Kahn SR, Lim W, Dunn AS, et al. Prevention of VTE in nonsurgical patients. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e195S-226S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60124-X/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315261?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   
               

               

                  
                  Orthopaedic surgery patients
               

               

                  
                  Prophylaxis (for a period of 10 to 14 days) with LMWH, fondaparinux, apixaban, dabigatran, rivaroxaban, low-dose unfractionated heparin, adjusted-dose vitamin K antagonist, aspirin, or IPC is recommended in patients undergoing elective hip or knee replacement surgery.
                   
                  [66] 
                  Falck-Ytter Y, Francis CW, Johanson NA, et al. Prevention of VTE in orthopedic surgery patients. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e278S-325S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60126-3/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315265?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   Thromboprophylaxis for up to 35 days from the day of surgery is recommended following major orthopaedic surgery.
                   
                  [66] 
                  Falck-Ytter Y, Francis CW, Johanson NA, et al. Prevention of VTE in orthopedic surgery patients. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e278S-325S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60126-3/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315265?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
               

               

                  
                  LMWH, fondaparinux, low-dose unfractionated heparin, adjusted-dose vitamin K antagonist, aspirin, or IPC (for a period of 10 to 14 days) is recommended for patients undergoing hip fracture surgery.
               

               

                  
                  Early mobilisation lowers risk of VTE following total hip replacement.
                   
                  [72] 
                  White RH, Gettner S, Newman JM, et al. Predictors of rehospitalization for symptomatic venous thromboembolism after total hip arthroplasty. N Engl J Med. 2000 Dec 14;343(24):1758-64. 
                  
                  https://www.nejm.org/doi/10.1056/NEJM200012143432403
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11114314?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
               

               

                  
                  General and abdominal-pelvic surgery
               

               

                  
                  Early mobilisation is recommended for patients at very low risk for VTE.
                   
                  [67] 
                  Gould MK, Garcia DA, Wren SM, et al. Prevention of VTE in nonorthopedic surgical patients. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e227S-77S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60125-1/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315263?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   Mechanical prophylaxis with IPC is recommended for low-risk general and abdominal-pelvic surgery patients.
               

               

                  
                  For general and abdominal-pelvic surgery patients who are at moderate risk for VTE but not at high risk for major bleeding complications, LMWH, low-dose unfractionated heparin, or IPC are recommended.
                   
                  [67] 
                  Gould MK, Garcia DA, Wren SM, et al. Prevention of VTE in nonorthopedic surgical patients. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e227S-77S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60125-1/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315263?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   Patients at moderate risk for VTE who are at high risk for major bleeding complications, or for whom the consequences of major bleeding would be severe, should receive mechanical prophylaxis with IPC.
                   
                  [73] 
                  Kakkos SK, Caprini JA, Geroulakos G, et al. Combined intermittent pneumatic leg compression and pharmacological prophylaxis for prevention of venous thromboembolism. Cochrane Database Syst Rev. 2016 Sep 7;(9):CD005258. 
                  
                  http://cochranelibrary-wiley.com/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD005258.pub3/full
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27600864?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   
               

               

                  
                  High-risk patients who are not at high risk for major bleeding complications should receive LMWH or low-dose unfractionated heparin, and mechanical prophylaxis (elastic stockings or IPC).
                   
                  [67] 
                  Gould MK, Garcia DA, Wren SM, et al. Prevention of VTE in nonorthopedic surgical patients. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e227S-77S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60125-1/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315263?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   Low-dose aspirin, fondaparinux, or mechanical prophylaxis are alternatives if LMWH and unfractionated heparin are contraindicated. IPC is recommended for patients at high risk for major bleeding complications, or those for whom the consequences of bleeding are thought to be severe. 
               

               

                  
                  Pregnancy
               

               

                  
                  LMWH is recommended for the prevention of VTE in pregnant women at increased risk of VTE.
                   
                  [64] 
                  Bates SM, Greer IA, Middeldorp S, et al. VTE, thrombophilia, antithrombotic therapy, and pregnancy. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e691S-736S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60136-6/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315276?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   For women who fulfil the laboratory and clinical (a history of 3 or more pregnancy losses) criteria for antiphospholipid syndrome, antepartum administration of prophylactic or intermediate-dose unfractionated heparin, or prophylactic LMWH combined with low-dose aspirin, is recommended. Antithrombotic prophylaxis is not recommended for: women with inherited thrombophilia and a history of pregnancy complications; or women with 2 or more miscarriages, but without antiphospholipid antibodies or thrombophilia.
                   
                  [64] 
                  Bates SM, Greer IA, Middeldorp S, et al. VTE, thrombophilia, antithrombotic therapy, and pregnancy. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e691S-736S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60136-6/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315276?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
               

               

                  
                  Cancer and surgical patients with cancer
               

               

                  
                  Prophylactic-dose LMWH or low-dose unfractionated heparin is recommended for outpatients with solid tumours who have additional risk factors for VTE and who are at low risk of bleeding.
                   
                  [65] 
                  Kahn SR, Lim W, Dunn AS, et al. Prevention of VTE in nonsurgical patients. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e195S-226S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60124-X/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315261?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
               

               

                  
                  Routine pharmacological prophylaxis is not recommended for outpatients with cancer who have no additional risk factors for VTE.
                   
                  [65] 
                  Kahn SR, Lim W, Dunn AS, et al. Prevention of VTE in nonsurgical patients. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e195S-226S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60124-X/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315261?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
               

               

                  
                  Patients at high risk of VTE who are undergoing abdominal or pelvic surgery for cancer, and who are not otherwise at high risk for major bleeding complications, should receive extended pharmacological prophylaxis (4 weeks) with LMWH.
                   
                  [67] 
                  Gould MK, Garcia DA, Wren SM, et al. Prevention of VTE in nonorthopedic surgical patients. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e227S-77S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60125-1/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315263?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
               

               

                  
                  Pharmacological thromboprophylaxis is not recommended in outpatients with cancer for the prevention of catheter-related thrombosis.
                   
                  [65] 
                  Kahn SR, Lim W, Dunn AS, et al. Prevention of VTE in nonsurgical patients. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e195S-226S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60124-X/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315261?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
               

               

                  
                  Cardiac and thoracic surgery
               

               

                  
                  Cardiac surgery patients with an uncomplicated postoperative course should receive mechanical prophylaxis, preferably with IPC.
                   
                  [67] 
                  Gould MK, Garcia DA, Wren SM, et al. Prevention of VTE in nonorthopedic surgical patients. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e227S-77S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60125-1/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315263?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   For those cardiac surgery patients whose hospital stay is prolonged by a non-haemorrhagic surgical complication, LMWH or low-dose unfractionated heparin can be added to mechanical prophylaxis.
               

               

                  
                  LMWH, low-dose unfractionated heparin, or IPC is recommended for thoracic surgery patients at moderate risk for VTE who are not at high risk for perioperative bleeding.
                   
                  [67] 
                  Gould MK, Garcia DA, Wren SM, et al. Prevention of VTE in nonorthopedic surgical patients. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e227S-77S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60125-1/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315263?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   For thoracic surgery patients at high risk for VTE who are not at high risk for perioperative bleeding, LMWH or low-dose unfractionated heparin, plus IPC or elastic stockings, is recommended. IPC is recommended for thoracic surgery patients at high risk for major bleeding.
               

               

                  
                  Major trauma patients
               

               

                  
                  LMWH, low-dose unfractionated heparin, or IPC is recommended for the prevention of VTE in major trauma patients.
                   
                  [67] 
                  Gould MK, Garcia DA, Wren SM, et al. Prevention of VTE in nonorthopedic surgical patients. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e227S-77S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60125-1/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315263?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   Pharmacological prophylaxis is recommended for patients at high risk for VTE (e.g., those with acute spinal cord injury, traumatic brain injury, and spinal surgery for trauma); mechanical prophylaxis can be added when not contraindicated by lower extremity injury.
                   
                  [73] 
                  Kakkos SK, Caprini JA, Geroulakos G, et al. Combined intermittent pneumatic leg compression and pharmacological prophylaxis for prevention of venous thromboembolism. Cochrane Database Syst Rev. 2016 Sep 7;(9):CD005258. 
                  
                  http://cochranelibrary-wiley.com/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD005258.pub3/full
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27600864?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   IPC is recommended (if not contraindicated) for major trauma patients in whom LMWH and low-dose unfractionated heparin are contraindicated.
                   
                  [67] 
                  Gould MK, Garcia DA, Wren SM, et al. Prevention of VTE in nonorthopedic surgical patients. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e227S-77S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60125-1/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315263?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   
               

               

                  
                  Craniotomy and spinal surgery
               

               

                  
                  IPC is recommended for the prevention of VTE in craniotomy and spinal surgery patients.
                   
                  [67] 
                  Gould MK, Garcia DA, Wren SM, et al. Prevention of VTE in nonorthopedic surgical patients. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e227S-77S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60125-1/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315263?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   For craniotomy and spinal surgery patients at very high risk for VTE (e.g., those with malignant disease), pharmacological prophylaxis can be added to mechanical prophylaxis when homeostasis is established and bleeding risk declines.
               

               

                  
                  Long-distance travel
               

               

                  
                  Neither aspirin nor anticoagulants is recommended for prevention of VTE in long-distance travellers.
                   
                  [65] 
                  Kahn SR, Lim W, Dunn AS, et al. Prevention of VTE in nonsurgical patients. Antithrombotic therapy and prevention of thrombosis, 9th ed: American College of Chest Physicians evidence-based clinical practice guidelines. Chest. 2012 Feb;141(2 Suppl):e195S-226S. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(12)60124-X/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22315261?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   Those travellers at increased risk of VTE (e.g., recent surgery or trauma, active malignancy, pregnancy) should: use properly fitted, below-knee graduated compression stockings providing 15 to 30 mmHg of pressure at the ankle during travel; move or walk about frequently; exercise calf muscles; and sit in an aisle seat (if possible).

                  [76]

Eur Heart J. 2014 Nov 14;35(43):3033-69. 
                  
                  https://academic.oup.com/eurheartj/article/35/43/3033/503581
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25173341?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   
                  [109] 
                  Kearon C, Akl EA, Ornelas J, et al; American College of Chest Physicians. Antithrombotic therapy for VTE disease: CHEST guideline and expert panel report. Chest. 2016 Feb;149(2):315-52. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(15)00335-9/fulltext

                        Unprovoked PE with high bleeding risk: 3 months.

                  Becattini C, Agnelli G. Risk stratification and management of acute pulmonary embolism. Hematology Am Soc Hematol Educ Program. 2016 Dec 2;2016(1):404-12. 
                  
                  http://asheducationbook.hematologylibrary.org/content/2016/1/404.long
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27913508?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   and there is usually no need to change the initial oral anticoagulant in patients who receive continued (>3 months) anticoagulation therapy.
                   
                  [109] 
                  Kearon C, Akl EA, Ornelas J, et al; American College of Chest Physicians. Antithrombotic therapy for VTE disease: CHEST guideline and expert panel report. Chest. 2016 Feb;149(2):315-52. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(15)00335-9/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26867832?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   However, reduced-intensity or low-intensity anticoagulation with rivaroxaban or apixaban significantly reduced the risk of recurrent events in patients with venous thromboembolism (VTE) who had previously completed 6 to 12 months of standard anticoagulation therapy (e.g., with a vitamin K antagonist, enoxaparin, or a direct-acting oral anticoagulant agent such as dabigatran, rivaroxaban, apixaban, or edoxaban).
                   
                  [240] 
                  Weitz JI, Lensing AW, Prins MH, et al; EINSTEIN CHOICE Investigators. Rivaroxaban or aspirin for extended treatment of venous thromboembolism. N Engl J Med. 2017 Mar 30;376(13):1211-22. 
                  
                  https://www.nejm.org/doi/10.1056/NEJMoa1700518
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28316279?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   
                  [241] 
                  Agnelli G, Buller HR, Cohen A, et al; AMPLIFY-EXT Investigators. Apixaban for extended treatment of venous thromboembolism. N Engl J Med. 2013 Feb 21;368(8):699-708. 
                  
                  https://www.nejm.org/doi/10.1056/NEJMoa1207541
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23216615?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   Neither rivaroxaban nor apixaban was associated with an increased risk of bleeding.
                   
                  [240] 
                  Weitz JI, Lensing AW, Prins MH, et al; EINSTEIN CHOICE Investigators. Rivaroxaban or aspirin for extended treatment of venous thromboembolism. N Engl J Med. 2017 Mar 30;376(13):1211-22. 
                  
                  https://www.nejm.org/doi/10.1056/NEJMoa1700518
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28316279?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   
                  [241] 
                  Agnelli G, Buller HR, Cohen A, et al; AMPLIFY-EXT Investigators. Apixaban for extended treatment of venous thromboembolism. N Engl J Med. 2013 Feb 21;368(8):699-708. 
                  
                  https://www.nejm.org/doi/10.1056/NEJMoa1207541
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23216615?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
               

               

                  
                  Recommendations for continuation of anticoagulant therapy vary by patient population. In all patients who receive extended anticoagulant therapy, its continued use should be re-assessed at periodic intervals (such as annually).
                   
                  [109] 
                  Kearon C, Akl EA, Ornelas J, et al; American College of Chest Physicians. Antithrombotic therapy for VTE disease: CHEST guideline and expert panel report. Chest. 2016 Feb;149(2):315-52. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(15)00335-9/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26867832?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
               

               

                  
                  Active cancer
               

               
    
                  
  • 
                     
    
                        
                        Low molecular weight heparin (LMWH) is preferred over a vitamin K antagonist (VKA), dabigatran, rivaroxaban, apixaban, or edoxaban for the treatment of PE in patients with active malignancy.
                         
                        [76] 
                        Konstantinides SV, Torbicki A, Agnelli G, et al; Task Force for the Diagnosis and Management of Acute Pulmonary Embolism of the European Society of Cardiology (ESC). 2014 ESC guidelines on the diagnosis and management of acute pulmonary embolism. Eur Heart J. 2014 Nov 14;35(43):3033-69. 
                        
                        https://academic.oup.com/eurheartj/article/35/43/3033/503581
                        
                        
                        http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25173341?tool=bestpractice.com
                        
                        
                         
                        [109] 
                        Kearon C, Akl EA, Ornelas J, et al; American College of Chest Physicians. Antithrombotic therapy for VTE disease: CHEST guideline and expert panel report. Chest. 2016 Feb;149(2):315-52. 
                        
                        https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(15)00335-9/fulltext
                        
                        
                        http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26867832?tool=bestpractice.com
                        
                        
                         
                        [211] 
                        Lyman GH, Bohlke K, Khorana AA, et al. Venous thromboembolism prophylaxis and treatment in patients with cancer: American Society of Clinical Oncology clinical practice guideline update 2014. J Clin Oncol. 2015 Feb 20;33(6):654-6. 
                        
                        http://ascopubs.org/doi/full/10.1200/JCO.2014.59.7351
                        
                        
                        http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25605844?tool=bestpractice.com
                        
                        
                         
                        
                        [  ] 
                        
                        How do low‐molecular‐weight heparin (LMWH), vitamin K agonists (VKAs), and direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) compare for treatment of venous thromboembolism (VTE) in people with cancer?
                        /cca.html?targetUrl=https://cochranelibrary.com/cca/doi/10.1002/cca.2255/full
                        Show me the answer
                        
                         European guidelines recommend anticoagulant therapy for a period of 3 to 6 months;
                         
                        [76] 
                        Konstantinides SV, Torbicki A, Agnelli G, et al; Task Force for the Diagnosis and Management of Acute Pulmonary Embolism of the European Society of Cardiology (ESC). 2014 ESC guidelines on the diagnosis and management of acute pulmonary embolism. Eur Heart J. 2014 Nov 14;35(43):3033-69. 
                        
                        https://academic.oup.com/eurheartj/article/35/43/3033/503581
                        
                        
                        http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25173341?tool=bestpractice.com
                        
                        
                         the American College of Chest Physicians (ACCP) recommends extended anticoagulant therapy (i.e., no scheduled stop date).
                         
                        [109] 
                        Kearon C, Akl EA, Ornelas J, et al; American College of Chest Physicians. Antithrombotic therapy for VTE disease: CHEST guideline and expert panel report. Chest. 2016 Feb;149(2):315-52. 
                        
                        https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(15)00335-9/fulltext
                        
                        
                        http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26867832?tool=bestpractice.com
                        
                        
                         LMWH is more effective than a VKA in patients with cancer (moderate-quality evidence), is therapeutically reliable in patients for whom oral therapy may be inappropriate (e.g., vomiting), and is easier to adjust/withhold if thrombocytopenia develops or an invasive intervention is needed.
                         
                        [109] 
                        Kearon C, Akl EA, Ornelas J, et al; American College of Chest Physicians. Antithrombotic therapy for VTE disease: CHEST guideline and expert panel report. Chest. 2016 Feb;149(2):315-52. 
                        
                        https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(15)00335-9/fulltext
                        
                        
                        http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26867832?tool=bestpractice.com
                        
                        
                         Furthermore, patients with cancer treated with a VKA may be difficult to maintain within the therapeutic range, and experience substantial rates of recurrence.
                         
                        [109] 
                        Kearon C, Akl EA, Ornelas J, et al; American College of Chest Physicians. Antithrombotic therapy for VTE disease: CHEST guideline and expert panel report. Chest. 2016 Feb;149(2):315-52. 
                        
                        https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(15)00335-9/fulltext
                        
                        
                        http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26867832?tool=bestpractice.com
                        
                        
                         
                     
    
                  
    • 
               

               

                  
                  Renal impairment
               

               
    
                  
  • 
                     
    
                        
                        The American College of Chest Physicians (ACCP) recommends a VKA for patients with severe renal impairment (i.e., creatinine clearance <30 mL/minute).
                         
                        [109] 
                        Kearon C, Akl EA, Ornelas J, et al; American College of Chest Physicians. Antithrombotic therapy for VTE disease: CHEST guideline and expert panel report. Chest. 2016 Feb;149(2):315-52. 
                        
                        https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(15)00335-9/fulltext
                        
                        
                        http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26867832?tool=bestpractice.com
                        
                        
                         Health professionals should refer to the label and/or local formularies before prescribing a direct-acting oral anticoagulant for a patient with renal impairment.
                     
    
                  
    • 
               

               

                  
                  Hepatic impairment and coagulopathy
               

               
    
                  
  • 
                     
    
                        
                        ACCP guidelines recommend LMWH in this patient population.
                         
                        [109] 
                        Kearon C, Akl EA, Ornelas J, et al; American College of Chest Physicians. Antithrombotic therapy for VTE disease: CHEST guideline and expert panel report. Chest. 2016 Feb;149(2):315-52. 
                        
                        https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(15)00335-9/fulltext
                        
                        
                        http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26867832?tool=bestpractice.com
                        
                        
                         Health professionals should refer to the label and/or local formularies before prescribing a direct-acting oral anticoagulant for a patient with hepatic impairment.
                     
    
                  
    • 
               

               

                  
                  Patients with recurrent VTE on anticoagulant therapy
               

               

                  
                  Recurrent VTE is unusual among patients receiving therapeutic-dose anticoagulant therapy.
                   
                  [109] 
                  Kearon C, Akl EA, Ornelas J, et al; American College of Chest Physicians. Antithrombotic therapy for VTE disease: CHEST guideline and expert panel report. Chest. 2016 Feb;149(2):315-52. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(15)00335-9/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26867832?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   In addition to definitively establishing the presence of recurrent PE, consideration should be given to compliance with anticoagulant therapy or the presence of underlying malignancy.
                   
                  [109] 
                  Kearon C, Akl EA, Ornelas J, et al; American College of Chest Physicians. Antithrombotic therapy for VTE disease: CHEST guideline and expert panel report. Chest. 2016 Feb;149(2):315-52. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(15)00335-9/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26867832?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   
               

               

                  
                  ACCP guidelines recommend a temporary switch to LMWH (for at least 1 month) for patients with recurrent PE who are thought to be compliant with a non-LMWH anticoagulant (or within the therapeutic range if receiving VKA therapy).
                   
                  [109] 
                  Kearon C, Akl EA, Ornelas J, et al; American College of Chest Physicians. Antithrombotic therapy for VTE disease: CHEST guideline and expert panel report. Chest. 2016 Feb;149(2):315-52. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(15)00335-9/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26867832?tool=bestpractice.com
                  
                  
                   An increased dose of LMWH (one quarter to one third) is appropriate for patients with recurrent PE who have been receiving LMWH.
                   
                  [109] 
                  Kearon C, Akl EA, Ornelas J, et al; American College of Chest Physicians. Antithrombotic therapy for VTE disease: CHEST guideline and expert panel report. Chest. 2016 Feb;149(2):315-52. 
                  
                  https://journal.chestnet.org/article/S0012-3692(15)00335-9/fulltext
                  
                  
                  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26867832?tool=bestpractice.com
