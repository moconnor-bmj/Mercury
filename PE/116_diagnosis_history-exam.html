History and exam

   
            
Key diagnostic factors

            
            

                  

                     

                        

                           

                              

                                 
                                 common
                                 
                                 VIEW ALL
                                 
                                 
                                 
                              

                           

                           
   
                           

                              

                                 
dyspnoea

                              

                           

                           

                              

                                    
Dyspnoea is the most common presenting symptom and is seen in 50% of patients with PE. [ESC] [NICE] 

                                       
    
                                       
  • May be acute and severe in central PE but is often mild and may be transient in small peripheral PE. [ESC]
    • 
                                       
  • Worsening dyspnoea may be the only symptom in patients with pre-existing heart failure or pulmonary disease. [ESC]
    • 
                                       
  • Tachypnoea (respiratory rate ≥20 breaths per minute) is a common presenting sign in patients with acute PE (21% to 39%). [POLLACK]
    • 
                                       
  • Symptoms of PE usually come on acutely rather than gradually
    • 
                                       

                                    
                              

                           

   
                           

                              

                                 
chest pain

                              

                           

                           

                              

                                    
Pleuritic chest pain is most common and seen at presentation in 39% of patients with PE. [ESC][NICE]

                                    
    
                                    
  • The pain is normally localised to one side of the chest and is unlikely to be central. This is usually caused by pleural irritation due to distal emboli causing pulmonary infarction. [STEIN] 
    • 
                                    
  • Normally acute onset.
    • 
                                    

                                    
Central PE may also present with angina-like chest pain but this is uncommon. [ESC]

                                    
    
                                    
  • This possibly reflects right ventricular (RV) ischaemia and requires differential diagnosis with acute coronary syndrome (ACS) or aortic dissection. [ESC]
    • 
                                    

   
                                       

                                          

                                             
Practical tip / Pitfall panel

                                             
Do not use chest pain on palpation of the chest wall to distinguish musculoskeletal chest pain from PE. [CP]

                                             
    
                                                
  • This sign is present in 20% of people with PE.
    • 
                                             

                                          

                                       

   
                              

                           

   
                           

                              

                                 
Signs of concurrent deep vein thrombosis

                              

                           

                           

                              

                                    
Typically pain and swelling in one leg, although both legs may be affected.[NICEDVT]

                                    
    
                                    
  • Examine the lower limbs for tenderness, changes to skin colour and temperature, and venous distension. [NICEDVT]
    • 
                                    
  • Features of concurrent DVT are present in 24% of patients with a diagnosed PE. [ESC]
    • 
                                    

                              

                           

                           

                                 

                                    
Hypoxaemia

                                 

                              

                              

                                 

                                       
Oxygen saturations <94% (or <88% in patients at risk of hypercapnic respiratory failure)

                                       
    
                                          
  • However up to 40% of patients have normal arterial oxygen saturation and 20% have a normal alveolar-arterial oxygen gradient. [ESC]
    • 
                                          
  • The patient may be hypoxic at rest if a massive PE is present or on only on exertion with a smaller PE.
    • 
                                       

                                       
                                 

                              

      
                              

                                 

                                    
Positive Wells (or Geneva) score

                                 

                              

                              

                                 

                                       
Use the Wells (or Geneva) Score to assess the likelihood of a PE in any non-pregnant patient who is haemodynamically stable. [ESC] [NICE]

                                       
    
                                       
  • This will categorise the patient into either ‘PE likely’ (Wells score > 4) or ‘PE unlikely’ (Wells score ≤4).[ESC] [NICE]
    • 
                                       
  • It can also be used to guide the need for further investigations.
                                       
      
                                       
    • If a patient has a ‘PE likely’ Wells (or Geneva) Score, start anticoagulation then arrange investigation with definitive imaging (CTPA if available)
      • 
                                       
    • If a patient has a ‘PE unlikely’ Wells (or Geneva) Score, order a D-dimer unless the patient fulfils all the Pulmonary Embolism Rule-Out Criteria (in which case a PE can be effectively ruled out and no further investigations are indicated). [NICE] [ESC] [ACP]
      • 
                                       
    
                                       
    • 
                                       

                                       
Do not use Wells or Geneva Scores in pregnancy.

PE can be ruled out in around 30% of patients who present to the emergency department with a suspected PE through a combination of clinical probability assessment using the Wells (or Geneva) score, plus D-dimer testing if indicated. [ESC]. 

                                    
    
                                          
  • This probability-based approach minimises the unnecessary use of potentially harmful computed tomographic angiography (CTPA) in patients who have a low probability of a PE. [ARN]
    • 
                                    

                                    

                                       

                                          
Practical tip / Pitfall panel

                                          
Wells Score is commonly used in the UK but an alternative called the Geneva score is also available.

                                          
    
                                             
  • Choose which score to use based on local protocols.
    • 
                                          

                                       

                                    
                   
                                    
                                    

                                       

                                          
                                         
                                          

                                             
In a systematic review PE was confirmed in 12% of patients classified as ‘PE unlikely’ and in 50% of patients classified as ‘PE likely.’  [CER].

                                             
    
                                             
  • Neither one of Wells or Geneva Score has been shown to be superior to the other. However, the Geneva score is based solely on objective clinical terms and may be more reproducible whereas Wells includes a subjective judgement of whether an alternative diagnosis is less likely than PE. [ESC]
    • 
                                             
  • Sensitivity of the Wells and Geneva Scores ranged from 88% to 96% and specificity from 48% to 53% when each clinical decision tool was validated in a primary care dataset. [HEN] 
    • 
                                             

                                          

                                       

                                    

                              

                              

      
                              

                                 

                                    
Failure to meet Pulmonary Embolism Rule-out Criteria (PERC)

                                 

                              

                              

                                 

                                       
Apply PERC to any patient classified as ‘PE unlikely’ based on Wells (or Geneva) Score.

                                       
    
                                       
  • No further investigation is indicated if a patient meets all the PERC criteria (a PE can effectively be ruled out). The risk for PE is considered to be lower than the risk of testing. [RAJA] [ACP]
    • 
                                       
  • Request D-dimer testing for any patient in whom the PERC criteria fail to rule out a PE (i.e. one of more criterion not fulfilled).
    • 
                                       
  • The PERC criteria are:
                                       
      
                                       
    • Age <50
      • 
                                       
    • HR <100 bpm
      • 
                                       
    • SaO₂ on room air ≥95%
      • 
                                       
    • No unilateral leg swelling
      • 
                                       
    • No haemoptysis
      • 
                                       
    • No recent surgery or trauma (≤4 weeks ago requiring treatment with general anesthesia)
      • 
                                       
    • No prior PE or DVT
      • 
                                       
    • No hormone use (oral contraceptives, hormone replacement or oestrogenic hormones use in male or female patients)
      • 
                                       
    
                                       
    • 
                                       

   
                                       

                                          

                                             
                                            
                                             

                                                
A large meta-analysis of 12 studies determined that the overall proportion of PEs missed by using PERC was only 0.3% (44 of 14,844 total cases).

                                                
    
                                                
  • The pooled sensitivity of PERC across all 12 studies was 97% (95% CI, 96% to 98%), and the pooled specificity was 22% (CI, 22% to 23%), indicating that 22% of D-dimer tests could have been safely avoided had the PERC approach been universally applied. [SINGH]
    •

Signs of shock/hypotension or severely reduced haemodynamic reserve

                                 

                              

                              

                                 

                                       
Assess for signs of shock or hypotension (ABCDE approach) as this can indicate a high-risk massive/central PE.

                                       
    
                                          
  • Seek senior support and escalate to critical care if a high-risk PE is suspected.[ESC][ACP]
    • 
                                          
  • A high-risk (haemodynamically unstable) PE is defined as a systolic blood pressure (SBP) <90mmHg or a SBP drop ≥ 40mmHg for >15 minutes, if not caused by a new-onset arrhythmia, hypovolaemia or sepsis. [ESC]
                                             
      
                                                   
    • Around 5% of patients with PE are haemodynamically unstable at presentation. [LAPORTE]
      • 
                                             
    
                                          
    • 
                                       

                                       
Check for:

                                       
    
                                          
  • Other features of shock [ESC][NICE]:
                                             
      
                                                   
    • Look for signs of altered cognition. Agitation and distress characterise early and milder stages, whereas unresponsiveness indicates more severe and advanced shock.
      • 
                                                   
    • Also check for cool extremities, mottled or ashen skin, slow capillary refill, clamminess and oliguria.
      • 
                                             
    
                                          
    • 
                                          
  • Tachycardia - Heart rate > 100 bpm.
                                             
      
                                                   
    • Tachycardia is an important sign of a possible high-risk PE even in the absence of other signs of shock
      • 
                                             
    
                                          
    • 
                                          
  • Acute right ventricular (RV) strain
                                             
      
                                                   
    • Look for an elevated jugular venous pressure, sternal heave or accentuated pulmonary component of S2, although this is uncommon. [JC]
      • 
                                             
    
                                          
    • 
                                          
  • Syncope or presyncope.
                                             
      
                                                   
        
                                                      
      • Present in 6% of patients with PE. [ESC]
        • 
                                                   
      
                                             
    
                                          
    • 
                                       

                                 

                              

                        

                     

                  

            

   
            

   
               
 
                     
                     
                     
Other diagnostic factors
            
                     

                        

                           

                              
                              

                                 

                                    
                                    common
                                    
                                    VIEW ALL
                                    
                                    
                                    
                                 

                              

                              

                                 

                                    
Cough

                                 

                              

                              

                                 

                                       
    
                                       
  • Present in 23% of patients with PE. [ESC
    • 
                                       
  • On auscultation of the lungs you may hear crepitations. [NICE]
                                       
      
                                 

                              

                        

                        

                              
                              

                                 

                                    
                                    uncommon
                                    
                                    VIEW ALL
                                    
                                    
                                    
                                 

                              

                              

                                 

                                    
Fever

                                 

                              

                              

                                 

                                       
Temperature >37.8°C. [BELL] 

                                          
    
                                          
  • Present in 10% of patients with PE. [ESC]
    • 
                                          

                                          
It is possible for PE and pneumonia to co-exist. Fever may be present in both but tends to be higher(>39°C) in pneumonia. [ STEIN PD]

                                 

                              

   
                              

                                    

                                       
Haemoptysis

                                    

                                 

                                 

                                    

                                          
Present in 8% of patients with PE. [NICE]

                                          
Distinguish true haemoptysis from bleeding arising from elsewhere.

                                          
    
                                          
  • It is commonly estimated that around half of patients presenting with haemoptysis do not have true haemoptysis. It is vital to take a clear and thorough history and examination to distinguish this.
                                          
      
                                          
    • Bleeding presenting as haemoptysis can arise from from the gastrointestinal tract, upper airways, gingiva or from epistaxis. 
      • 
                                          
    
                                          
    •
