Recommendations

         

            

               

                  

                     

                        
Urgent

                        
    
                           
  • 
                              Assess haemodynamic status immediately on presentation, looking for early signs of fluid loss.[2] [7]
                              
      
                                 
    • Early administration of intravenous fluids is of critical importance in all patients with acute pancreatitis, however mild.
      • 
                                 
    • Evidence suggests it reduces the risk of organ failure and death
      • 
                              
    
                           
    • 
                           
  • 
                              Beware SIRS and/or multiorgan failure - these are the biggest risk to life in the first week.[2] [7] [8]
                              
      
                                 
    • Assess for signs of organ dysfunction, particularly cardiovascular, respiratory or renal.
      • 
                                 
    • Consider ITU transfer for any patient who has SIRS or early signs of organ failure.
      • 
                              
    
                           
    • 
                           
  • Arrange emergency ERCP (within 24 hours) for any patient with gallstone pancreatitis who has concurrent cholangitis.
    • 
                        

                     

                     
 Acute pancreatitis is associated with high rates of morbidity and mortality. The risk of death in patients with infected pancreatic necrosis is as high as 30%.

                     
Initial supportive treatment

                     
    
                        
  • Initial treatment consists of early goal-directed fluid resuscitation with Ringer's lactate or normal saline plus adequate pain control.
    • 
                        
  • 
                           Establish oral feeding as soon as the patient can tolerate it; do not keep patients Nil by Mouth for any longer than is absolutely necessary (e.g. vomiting, ileus).
                           
      
                              
    • Beware the traditional dogma of 'pancreatic rest'. Current evidence shows early oral feeding leads to better outcomes, regardless of disease severity.
      • 
                           
    
                        
    • 
                        
  • 
                           In patients with severe disease who cannot feed orally, use enteral feeding (either nasojejunal or nasogastric) rather than parenteral nutrition.
                           
      
                              
    • Enteral feeding is associated with lower rates of organ failure, pancreatic necrosis and death.
      • 
                           
    
                        
    • 
                        
  • Do not give prophylactic antibiotics. There is no evidence to support their use in patients without clear evidence or clinical suspicion of infection. This holds true for patients with sterile necrosis and those with severe disease.
    • 
                     

                     
Gallstone pancreatitis

                     
    
                        
  •  Arrange emergency ERCP (within 24 hours) for any patient who has concurrent cholangitis.  Suspect this based on Charcot‚Äôs triad.
    • 
                        
  •  ERCP is probably indicated in gallstone pancreatitis with bile duct obstruction.  Prior bile duct imaging with EUS (or MRCP) can prevent unnecessary ERCP procedures.
    • 
                        
  • Ensure cholecystectomy is performed during the same admission for any patient with mild gallstone pancreatitis.
    • 
                        
  • In severe gallstone pancreatitis, cholecystectomy is usually delayed for at least 6 weeks until local inflammation has resolved.
    • 
                     

                     
Alcohol-related pancreatitis

                     
    
                        
  • Ensure the patient has support from an  alcohol abstinence programme, starting with a brief intervention during the initial admission.
    • 
                     

                     
Local complications

                     
    
                        
  •  Do not request CECT scanning to detect local complications in the first 3-4 days after presentation.  Pancreatic necrosis often takes > 72 hours to develop so early CECT is unreliable.
    • 
                        
  •  Most patients with sterile necrosis can be managed conservatively. 
    • 
                        
  • Some patients with infected necrosis will recover with solely intravenous antibiotics and supportive care.
    • 
                        
  • 
                           Where further intervention is needed, this should be delayed if at all possible until the necrosis has become walled off (4 weeks).
                           
      
                              
    • The step-up approach is the preferred strategy for treating infected pancreatic necrosis. Start with catheter drainage (percutaneous or endoscopic) and only step up to necrosectomy if drainage is unsuccessful.
      • 
                              
    • Minimally invasive necrosectomy techniques are preferred to open surgery although further research is needed on different sub-groups of patients.
      • 
                           
    
                        
    • 
                     

                     
Assessing severity

                     
    
                        
  •  Organ failure is of paramount importance in assessing severity.
    • 
                        
  • 
                           During the first 48 hours, use SIRS and patient risk factors to predict severity.
                           
      
                              
    • Never classify a patient as having mild disease until at least 48 hours after onset of symptoms. Many patients who go on to develop severe disease present without signs of organ failure or local complications.
      • 
                              
    • Around 50% of patients will have predicted severe pancreatitis but only half of these will go on to develop moderately severe or severe disease.
      • 
                           
    
                        
    • 
                        
  • During admission, assess severity based on persistent SIRS and the absence/presence of local complications.
    • 
                        
  • Severity scoring tools such as APACHEII and Glasgow have limited value and are not recommended by evidence-based guidelines.
