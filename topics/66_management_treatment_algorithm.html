                                                        1st line
                                                         – 
                                            

                                            
                                                        
                                            initial resuscitation
                                            
                                            
                                            
                                        
                                    

                                


                                


                                    

                                    

                                    

                                        

                                        Intravenous hydration with Ringer’s lactate (Hartmann’s) or normal saline is essential.

                                        
                                            [63]
                                            Crockett SD, Wani S, Gardner
                                                TB, et al. American Gastroenterological Association Institute guideline
                                                on initial management of acute pancreatitis. Gastroenterology. 2018
                                                Mar;154(4):1096-101.

                                        
                                                http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29409760?tool=bestpractice.com
                                            

                                        

                                    


                                    

                                        

                                        Start intravenous fluid therapy with 5-10 mL/kg/hour and then proceed with goal-directed fluid therapy (the titration of fluid therapy to specific biochemical and clinical targets of perfusion such as heart rate <120 beats per minute, mean arterial pressure 65-85 mmHg [8.7-11.3 kPa], urinary output >0.5-1 mL/kg/hour, haematocrit 35% to 44%).

                                        
                                            [36]
                                            Working Group IAP/APA Acute
                                                Pancreatitis Guidelines. IAP/APA evidence-based guidelines for the
                                                management of acute pancreatitis. Pancreatology. 2013 Jul-Aug;13(4
                                                Suppl 2):e1-15.
                                        
                                                https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1424390313005255?via%3Dihub
                                            

                                        
                                                http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24054878?tool=bestpractice.com
                                            

                                        

                                        
                                            [63]
                                            Crockett SD, Wani S, Gardner
                                                TB, et al. American Gastroenterological Association Institute guideline
                                                on initial management of acute pancreatitis. Gastroenterology. 2018
                                                Mar;154(4):1096-101.

                                        
                                                http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29409760?tool=bestpractice.com
                                            

                                        

                                    


                                    

                                        

                                        In severe acute pancreatitis, the patient should be catheterised to monitor urinary output.

                                    


                                    

                                        

                                        In haemorrhagic pancreatitis, blood transfusion may be necessary.

                                            Basurto Ona X, Rigau Comas D,
                                                Urrútia G. Opioids for acute pancreatitis pain. Cochrane Database Syst

                                        Fentanyl or morphine can be used, either for breakthrough pain or as patient-controlled analgesia. In mild cases, the standard World Health Organization pain ladder can be used to inform the selection, monitoring, and adjustment of analgesia.

                                    


                                    

                                        

                                        Ketorolac, a non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drug, can be used in patients with intact renal function. It should not be used in older patients because of the risk of adverse gastrointestinal effects.

                                        
                                            [66]
                                            American Geriatrics Society
                                                2012 Beers Criteria Update Expert Panel. American Geriatrics Society

                                                        Plus
                                                         – 
                                            

                                            
                                                        
                                            nutritional support
                                            
                                            
                                            
                                        
                                    

                                


                                


                                    

                                    
Treatment recommended for ALL patients in selected patient group
                                    


                                    

                                    

                                        

                                        Oral nutrition should resume as soon as pain and any nausea/vomiting begin to subside. The American Gastroenterological Association recommends oral feeding within 24 hours, as tolerated, in patients with acute pancreatitis.

                                        
                                            [63]
                                            Crockett SD, Wani S, Gardner
                                                TB, et al. American Gastroenterological Association Institute guideline
                                                on initial management of acute pancreatitis. Gastroenterology. 2018
                                                Mar;154(4):1096-101.

                                        
                                                http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29409760?tool=bestpractice.com
                                            

                                        

                                    


                                    

                                        

                                        In patients who are unable to resume oral intake, enteral nutrition is preferred to parenteral nutrition.

                                        
                                            [63]
                                            Crockett SD, Wani S, Gardner
                                                TB, et al. American Gastroenterological Association Institute guideline
                                                on initial management of acute pancreatitis. Gastroenterology. 2018
                                                Mar;154(4):1096-101.

                                        
                                                http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29409760?tool=bestpractice.com
                                            

                                        

                                        Enteral nutrition has been associated with lower complication and morbidity rates.

                                        
                                            [68]
                                            Yi F, Ge L, Zhao J, et al.
                                                Meta-analysis: total parenteral nutrition versus total enteral
                                                nutrition in predicted severe acute pancreatitis. Intern Med.
                                                2012;51(6):523-30.
                                        
                                                https://www.jstage.jst.go.jp/article/internalmedicine/51/6/51_6_523/_pdf
                                            

                                        
                                                http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22449657?tool=bestpractice.com
                                            

                                        

                                        
                                            [69]
                                            Zuo YY, Kang Y. Total enteral
                                                nutrition versus total parenteral nutrition for patients with severe
                                                acute pancreatitis: a meta-analysis. Chinese J Evid Med.
                                                2011;11:1295-301.

                                        

                                         The nasogastric or nasoenteral (nasoduodenal or nasojejunal) routes can be used; however, the risk of aspiration may preclude nasogastric tubes in patients with severe acute pancreatitis.

                                        
                                            [63]
                                            Crockett SD, Wani S, Gardner
                                                TB, et al. American Gastroenterological Association Institute guideline
                                                on initial management of acute pancreatitis. Gastroenterology. 2018
                                                Mar;154(4):1096-101.

                                        
                                                http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29409760?tool=bestpractice.com
                                            

                                        

                                        The nasojejunal route allows enteral nutrition without stimulating the pancreas.

                                    


                                    

                                        

                                        Parenteral nutrition should be reserved for patients who do not tolerate enteral feeding or in whom an adequate infusion cannot be reached within 2 to 4 days. When compared with parenteral nutrition, enteral feeds are associated with better outcomes, less mortality, and better blood glucose control. They also protect the gut barrier by preventing intestinal atrophy, leading to less sepsis and fewer infectious complications.

                                    


                                    

                                        

                                        The recommended nutrient requirements in severe acute pancreatitis are as follows: energy 25 to 35 kcal/kg/day, protein 1.2 to 1.5 g/kg/day, carbohydrates 3 to 6 g/kg/day, and lipids 2 g/kg/day.

                                        
                                            [72]
                                            Mirtallo JM, Forbes A,
                                                McClave SA, et al. International consensus guidelines for nutrition
                                                therapy in pancreatitis. JPEN J Parenter Enteral Nutr. 2012
                                                May;36(3):284-91.

                                        
                                                http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22457421?tool=bestpractice.com
                                            

                                        

                                        
                                            [73]
                                            Meier R, Beglinger C, Layer
                                                P, et al; European Society of Parenteral and Enteral Nutrition. ESPEN
                                                guidelines on nutrition in acute pancreatitis. Clin Nutr. 2002
                                                Apr;21(2):173-83.

                                        
                                                http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12056792?tool=bestpractice.com
                                            

                                        

                                        
                                            [74]
                                            Gianotti L, Meier R, Lobo DN,
                                                et al; ESPEN. ESPEN guidelines on parenteral nutrition: pancreas. Clin
                                                Nutr. 2009 Aug;28(4):428-35.

                                        
                                                http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19464771?tool=bestpractice.com

Treatment recommended for SOME patients in selected patient group


                                    

                                    

                                        

                                        It is important to monitor arterial oxygenation, because patients may be hypoxaemic, requiring supplemental oxygen.

                                    


                                    

                                        

                                        Patients with acute pancreatitis are at high risk of hypoxia because of one or more of abdominal splinting, atelectasia, pulmonary oedema, or acute respiratory distress syndrome.

                                    


                                    

                                        

                                        During the initial management, consider arterial blood gases every 12 hours for the first 3 days to assess both oxygenation and acid-base status.

                                        
                                            [64]
                                            Way LW, Doherty GM. Chapter
                                                27: Pancreas. In: Current surgical diagnosis & treatment. 11th ed.
                                                New York, NY: McGraw-Hill; 2003.

                                        Blood glucose control and insulin administration to keep glucose <8.33 mmol/L (<150 mg/dL) has been associated with reductions in morbidity and mortality in critically ill patients.

                                    


                                    

                                        

                                        Insulin sliding scales, insulin drips, or long-acting insulin should be used with hyperglycaemia that is difficult to treat.

                                            Webster PD, Spainhour JB.

                                        See local specialist protocol for dosing guidelines.

Treatment recommended for ALL patients in selected patient group
                                    


                                    

                                        
                                        
                                        
Primary options


                                        


                                            


                                                thiamine: 100
                                                    mg orally/intravenously/intramuscularly once daily

                                            


                                            
and


                                            


                                                folic acid: 1
                                                    mg orally/intramuscularly once daily

                                            


                                            
and


                                            


                                                cyanocobalamin: 1000
                                                    micrograms intramuscularly/orally once daily for 1-2 weeks,
                                                    followed by 1000 micrograms once every 1-3 months

                                            


                                        

                                        

                                    

                                    

                                    

                                        

                                        The objective of replacement of thiamine in chronic alcoholism is to replenish the stores in patients. The treatment has to be continued until the patient can return to eating a well-balanced meal during hospitalisation.

                                    


                                    

                                        

                                        Other water-soluble vitamins that are supplemented during hospital or emergency department course include folic acid and cyanocobalamin.

                                    


                                    

                                        

                                        Cyanocobalamin can be given orally except in states when absorption is impaired.

                                    


                                

                                

                            


                            


                                

                                    

                                        

                                            
                                                
                                                    with infected pancreatic necrosis
                                                
                                             – 
                                            

                                            
                                                
                                                    
                                                        Plus
                                                         – 
                                            

                                            
                                                        
                                            intravenous antibiotics
                                            
                                            
                                            
                                        
                                    

                                


                                


                                    

                                    
Treatment recommended for ALL patients in selected patient group
                                    


                                    

                                        
                                        
                                        
Primary options


                                        


                                            


                                                imipenem/cilastatin:
                                                500-1000 mg intravenously every 6 hours

                                            


                                            
                                                More
                                                
                                                    imipenem/cilastatin
                                                
                                            

                                                    

                                                        

                                                        Dose refers to imipenem component only.

                                                    


                                                
                                            

                                        

                                        

                                    


                                    

                                        
                                        
                                        
Secondary options


                                        


                                            


                                                ciprofloxacin: 400
                                                    mg intravenously every 12 hours

                                            


                                        

                                        

                                        
OR


                                        


                                            


                                                metronidazole: 500
                                                    mg intravenously every 8 hours

                                            


                                        

                                        

                                        
OR


                                        


                                            


                                                ceftriaxone: 1-2
                                                    g intravenously every 12 hours

                                            


                                        

                                        

                                    

                                    

                                    

                                        

                                        All patients with confirmed or highly suspected infected necrosis should receive immediate intravenous antibiotics.

                                        
                                            [36]
                                            Working Group IAP/APA Acute
                                                Pancreatitis Guidelines. IAP/APA evidence-based guidelines for the
                                                management of acute pancreatitis. Pancreatology. 2013 Jul-Aug;13(4
                                                Suppl 2):e1-15.
                                        
                                                https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1424390313005255?via%3Dihub

                                        Imipenem/cilastatin is usually the first choice because it has good pancreatic penetration. Alternatives include a fluoroquinolone, metronidazole, or a high-dose cephalosporin.

                                        
                                            [39]
                                            Tenner S, Baillie J, DeWitt
                                                J, et al. American College of Gastroenterology guideline: management of
                                                acute pancreatitis. Am J Gastroenterol. 2013 Sep;108(9):1400-15.

                                                    with infected pancreatic necrosis
                                                
                                             – 
                                            

                                            
                                                
                                                    
                                                        Adjunct
                                                         – 
                                            

                                            
                                                        
                                            catheter drainage (percutaneous or
                                                            endoscopic)
                                            
                                            
                                            
                                        
                                    

                                


                                


                                    
                                    
Treatment recommended for SOME patients in selected patient group


                                    

                                    

                                        

                                        In general, patients who fail to respond to intravenous antibiotics are suitable for a step-up approach, starting with image-guided catheter drainage. Up to 30% of patients may be definitively managed by percutaneous drainage alone.

                                            Working Group IAP/APA Acute
                                                Pancreatitis Guidelines. IAP/APA evidence-based guidelines for the
                                                management of acute pancreatitis. Pancreatology. 2013 Jul-Aug;13(4
                                                Suppl 2):e1-15.

                                        Endoscopic debridement may be considered as a first approach in highly selected patients treated at experienced high-volume pancreatic centres.

                                    


                                

                                

                            


                            


                                

                                    

                                        

                                            
                                                
                                                    with infected pancreatic necrosis
                                                
                                             – 
                                            

                                            
                                                
                                                    
                                                        Adjunct
                                                         – 
                                            

                                            
                                                        
                                            necrosectomy/debridement
                                            
                                            
                                            
                                        
                                    

                                


                                


                                    
                                    
Treatment recommended for SOME patients in selected patient group


                                    

                                    

                                        

                                        Necrosectomy is typically reserved for those patients who do not respond to drainage.

                                            Working Group IAP/APA Acute
                                                Pancreatitis Guidelines. IAP/APA evidence-based guidelines for the
                                                management of acute pancreatitis. Pancreatology. 2013 Jul-Aug;13(4
                                                Suppl 2):e1-15.

                                        Very-low-quality evidence suggests that the minimally invasive step-up approach (initial drainage followed by laparoscopic necrosectomy if necessary) may be associated with fewer adverse events and serious adverse events than open necrosectomy.

                                                    with sterile pancreatic necrosis
                                                
                                             – 
                                            

                                            
                                                
                                                    
                                                        Adjunct
                                                         – 
                                            

                                            
                                                        
                                            catheter drainage or necrosectomy
                                            
                                            
                                            
                                        
                                    

                                


                                


                                    
                                    
Treatment recommended for SOME patients in selected patient group


                                    

                                    

                                        

                                        Most patients can be managed conservatively.

                                        Indications for radiological, surgical, or endoscopic intervention in sterile necrosis include: ongoing gastric outlet, intestinal, or biliary obstruction due to the mass effect of walled-off necrosis (normally 4-8 weeks after the onset of acute pancreatitis); symptoms (e.g., pain, general unwellness) that persist beyond 8 weeks after the onset of acute pancreatitis; disconnected duct syndrome with persistent symptomatic collections beyond 8 weeks after the onset of acute pancreatitis.
